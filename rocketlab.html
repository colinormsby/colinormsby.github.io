01
Investment Thesis

Three Engines Driving a Step-Change in Valuation

Rocket Lab's Q1 2026 results were not just a beat — they represent a full regime change in how this company should be valued. Record revenue of $200.3M (+63.5% YoY) surpassed every guidance metric. Space Systems delivered $136.7M, now comprising 68% of total revenue, while a $2.2B backlog — up 108% year-over-year — provides exceptional multi-year visibility. The company exited Q1 with $1.48B in cash and access to $2B+ in total liquidity.

The launch segment is equally compelling. Rocket Lab booked 31 Electron and HASTE missions in a single quarter — more launches than it signed in all of 2024 — and now counts 87 completed Electron missions with 100% mission success in 2025. The company is on track for its historic 100th launch in 2026, cementing its place as the world's second most active launch provider. In Q1, it also signed 5 Neutron contracts and closed the largest launch deal in company history with a confidential customer.

Neutron is the variant perception story the market has not yet fully priced. Archimedes engine testing is progressing; Launch Complex 3 at Wallops is under construction. We model first flight in H2 2026 with commercial revenue contribution in 2027. Medium-lift is structurally underserved — Falcon 9 has an 18-month manifest backlog — and Rocket Lab's existing government relationships remove the highest-friction part of the sales cycle.

Wall Street consensus sits at $87–$105 per share. We see material upside from this range because consensus models underweight the optionality of Neutron at scale and the compounding moat effect of vertical integration. At 12-month target, RKLB is valued as a space services platform — not a launch company — commanding multiples consistent with high-growth infrastructure peers.

03
Segment Analysis
01 · Primary Segment
Space Systems
$136.7M Q1 2026
68% of revenue · 58.5% of backlog

The backbone of Rocket Lab's recurring revenue model. Spacecraft components — reaction wheels, solar panels, star trackers, separation systems — are sold to third-party satellite builders globally, creating a predictable, high-margin annuity with multi-year contract visibility.

SDA Tranche 2 spacecraft manufacturing contracts underpin the backlog. Civil and commercial constellation builds extend delivery schedules to 2028+. Space Systems gross margins are expanding as scale benefits kick in and vertical integration eliminates external sourcing costs.

02 · Launch Vehicle
Launch Services
$63.7M Q1 2026
32% of revenue · 41.5% of backlog

Electron is the world's most frequently-flown small dedicated launch vehicle with 87 missions completed and 100% mission success in 2025. Rocket Lab booked 31 Electron and HASTE missions in Q1 alone — more than all of 2024 — and now has 70+ launches in its launch backlog, including a $190M MACH-TB 2.0 HASTE contract (20 launches).

The production line is designed for up to 52 Electron launches annually. Modest incremental investment can push cadence higher — pure margin expansion from here.

03 · Emerging
Neutron
5 contracts signed Q1
First flight target: H2 2026

A 13-ton LEO medium-lift reusable rocket with Archimedes (LOX/methane) engines. First stage designed for propulsive recovery and reuse. 5 Neutron contracts were signed in Q1 2026 — unprecedented for a pre-first-flight vehicle.

Target ASP $50–80M per mission. Three flights in 2027 alone would generate $150–240M in incremental revenue — transformational for EBITDA at a company where fixed costs are largely covered by Space Systems.

04
Catalysts In Detail
01
M&A · Vertical Integration
Acquisitions Compound the Moat

Rocket Lab has executed eight acquisitions since 2021 — each adding proprietary component capability: SolAero Technologies (high-efficiency solar cells), Advanced Solutions Inc. (GNC software), Planetary Systems Corp (separation systems), and Sinclair Interplanetary (reaction wheels).

This vertical integration strategy eliminates supply chain single points of failure, increases Space Systems margins (components sold externally at markup), and creates a deep technical moat that cannot be replicated quickly by pure-play launch competitors.

We expect 1–2 additional bolt-on acquisitions in 2026, likely targeting ground systems or on-orbit servicing capability.

02
Neutron · Strategic Analysis
The Medium-Lift Gap Is Massive

The global commercial launch market is projected to reach $30B+ by 2030. Medium-lift — vehicles in the 5–15 ton to LEO class — is the most structurally underserved segment. Falcon 9 dominates but has 12–18 month manifest backlog; ULA's Vulcan is operationally constrained; Ariane 6 serves European institutional demand.

Neutron enters a market where qualified, reliable, U.S.-origin medium lift capacity commands a scarcity premium. Government customers — NRO and DoD — have made vendor diversification a stated policy goal. Rocket Lab's existing trust removes a critical sales cycle barrier.

We model 3 Neutron flights in 2027, scaling to 8–10 by 2029, generating $400–800M in annual revenue at scale.

Risk Matrix
Severity Risk Factor Detail Mitigation
High Neutron Delay Hardware-intensive launch vehicle development is inherently schedule-risky. A 12–18 month slip would delay revenue recognition and could necessitate additional capital raises, compressing multiple. Cash runway ≥18 months; phased testing reduces binary failure risk.
High Competition Intensifies SpaceX rideshare, Firefly Aerospace, ABL Space, and international entrants compete for small-sat launch and components. Starship's expanding cadence may commoditize launch economics. Vertical integration, dedicated launch premium, and U.S.-only supply chain differentiate RKLB.
Med Government Budget Risk SDA, NRO, and civil agency budgets are subject to continuing resolution and appropriations risk. A DoD spending reset could delay tranche awards and slow backlog conversion. Backlog provides 3–4Q forward coverage; commercial diversification reduces concentration.
Med Dilution Risk Neutron capex could require $300–500M in additional funding. Equity raises would be dilutive to existing shareholders. However, at record Q1 2026 revenue and $1.48B cash on hand, the near-term risk is significantly reduced vs. prior periods. $1.48B cash + $2B+ total liquidity provides substantial runway. Management has signalled preference for non-dilutive government grants and off-take contracts.
Low Electron Mission Failure A high-profile failure could delay manifest, damage commercial reputation, and trigger insurance/warranty costs affecting near-term revenue. Fleet data and mission assurance processes mature with each flight. Current success rate >90%.
Low Export Controls ITAR and EAR exposure limits international commercial customers. Policy changes could constrain NZ-based launches. Wallops Island launch site operational and expanding. Launch license diversification underway.
